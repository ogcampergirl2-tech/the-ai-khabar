AI “agents,” explained

2025 was the year of the chatbot. 2026 is the year everyone started saying “agent.” Here's the real difference, and what you can safely try yourself.

Picture the difference between a knowledgeable friend who answers your questions, and an assistant who actually goes and gets things done. The first is a chatbot. The second is an agent. That one shift, from telling you to doing for you, is the whole story behind the word everyone is suddenly using.

A regular chatbot is a brilliant conversationalist trapped in a box. You ask, it answers, and that's the end of its world. An AI agent takes the same underlying model and hands it three new powers: the ability to make a plan, to use tools, and to keep going until a goal is met. Same brain, far more reach.

The three ingredients of an agent

Whenever you hear “agent,” you can mentally check for these three things. If they're present, you're looking at an agent rather than a chatbot.

  • A goal, not just a question. “Book me the cheapest flight to Delhi next Friday” instead of “What flights go to Delhi?”
  • Tools it can actually use. Searching the web, running code, sending an email, reading your files, clicking buttons on a website. The model decides which tool to reach for, and when.
  • A loop. It tries a step, looks at the result, decides what to do next, and repeats, until the job is done or it gets stuck. This “think → act → observe → repeat” cycle is the heart of the whole idea.
A chatbot finishes when it has spoken. An agent finishes when the task is done.

A concrete example

Say you ask an agent to “find three good cafés near the office for a team lunch and draft an email inviting everyone.” A chatbot would give you generic suggestions from memory. An agent would: search the web for current options, check that they're open and well-reviewed, pick three, and then write the invitation, adjusting along the way if a place turned out to be closed. Several steps, several tools, one instruction from you.

Why now?

Agents aren't new in theory; the idea is decades old. What changed is that the underlying models finally got reliable enough at reasoning and tool use to make the loop work without falling apart. That tipping point is what turned 2026 into “the year of agents.”

Where agents are genuinely useful today

Cutting through the hype, a few areas are already delivering real value:

  • Coding. Agents that read a codebase, make a change across many files, run the tests, and fix what broke. This is the most mature use by far.
  • Research & summarising. “Look into X across these ten sources and give me a briefing”: gathering and condensing is a natural fit.
  • Repetitive digital chores. Sorting an inbox, filling forms, reconciling two spreadsheets, moving data between apps.

And where they still stumble

It would be a disservice to oversell them. Agents still get confused on long, fiddly tasks. They can confidently take a wrong turn and march several steps in the wrong direction before noticing. And the more power you hand an agent (your email, your files, your money) the more a small mistake can cost. The sensible rule for 2026: let agents draft and do, but keep a human at the steering wheel for anything important or irreversible.

A word on safety

Start agents on low-stakes tasks where a mistake is cheap and reversible. Watch what they do. Graduate them to bigger responsibilities only once you trust the pattern. This is exactly how you'd train a new intern, and it's the right instinct here too.

The one-sentence version

Next time someone mentions agents, you can say: “An agent is an AI that doesn't just answer. It's given a goal, a set of tools, and the freedom to take several steps on its own to get the job done.” That's the whole concept, and now you've got it. ■
